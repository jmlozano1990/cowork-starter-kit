# Skills as Prompts — Business/Admin Preset

Use this file if skill upload is not available. Copy the skill content below and paste it at the start of your message to Cowork:

"Using this approach: [paste skill content] — now help me with [your task]."

---

## Email Drafting

When drafting a professional email, follow this approach: Ask for: (1) the recipient and their relationship to the sender (internal colleague, external client, executive, vendor, etc.); (2) the desired outcome — what should happen after the recipient reads this email; (3) the tone — formal, warm, or direct. If not specified, calibrate to the recipient relationship. Then draft the email with: a clear subject line, an opening that gets to the point in the first sentence, a body that contains only what is necessary, and a clear call to action or next step. Under 200 words unless the content requires more. Do not include filler phrases ("I hope this email finds you well", "As per my previous email"). Always output the subject line separately from the body. Offer a shorter or more direct variant if the first draft feels long.

**Example prompts:**

- "Draft an email declining a meeting request while keeping the relationship warm."
- "Write a follow-up email to a client who hasn't responded in two weeks."
- "Draft a direct email to my team about the deadline change for [project]."

## Report Summary

When summarizing a report or document, follow this approach: Read the full document first. Identify the single most important finding or recommendation — this becomes the headline. Then produce: (1) Headline insight — one sentence on the most important takeaway; (2) Supporting points — 3-5 bullet points on the key findings or data points that support the headline; (3) Recommended action — if the document implies an action or decision, state it explicitly in one sentence; (4) What to read if you want more — note the most relevant section for a reader who has 5 extra minutes. Keep the entire summary under 150 words. The goal is to enable a decision, not to comprehensively summarize the document.

**Example prompts:**

- "Summarize this report: [filename or paste text]."
- "What is the key finding in this document? [filename or paste]."
- "Give me a one-paragraph executive summary of [document]."

## Action Items

When identifying action items from a document, meeting, or email thread, follow this approach: Read all provided content. Identify every explicit or implied action — something that needs to be done. For each action item, produce: (1) a clear one-sentence description of the action; (2) the owner (who is responsible — infer from context if stated); (3) the deadline (if stated or implied). Format as a numbered list. Do not include items that are discussion points, not actions. If an action is ambiguous, note it as "Unclear owner — needs assignment" rather than assigning it to someone arbitrarily. End with a count of items with no owner or no deadline, so the user knows what needs to be resolved.

**Example prompts:**

- "What are the action items from this meeting? [paste notes or transcript]."
- "What files are in my Inbox/ folder? Draft a prioritized action list for today."
- "Extract all action items from this email thread: [paste thread]."
