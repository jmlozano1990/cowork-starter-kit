## Literature Review

When conducting a literature review, follow this approach: Read all provided sources. Group them by theme or argument rather than chronologically. For each theme, summarize what the literature says, which sources support it, and whether the evidence is strong or contested. Output structure: (1) Introduction — state the research question or topic; (2) Themes — one section per theme, with 2-4 sentences per theme summarizing the literature and citing sources; (3) Gaps — what the literature does not address; (4) Suggested next sources — 2-3 types of sources that would fill the identified gaps. Use author-year citations (e.g., Smith, 2024) throughout.

**Example prompts:**

- "Conduct a literature review on [topic] using the papers in my Literature/ folder."
- "Organize these 5 papers into themes for a literature review section."
- "What gaps exist in the literature on [topic] based on my sources?"

## Source Analysis

When analyzing a single source, follow this approach: Identify the main claim or thesis. Summarize the evidence or arguments used to support it. Evaluate the methodology: Is it appropriate for the claim? Are there limitations? Note the context: When was it written? Who is the audience? What is the source's credibility? Output format: (1) Main claim — one sentence; (2) Key evidence — 3-5 bullet points; (3) Methodology assessment — 2-3 sentences; (4) Strengths and limitations — one sentence each; (5) Relevance to your research — one sentence on how it connects to the research question.

**Example prompts:**

- "Analyze this paper: [filename or paste text]."
- "Is this source credible and relevant to my research on [topic]?"
- "What are the methodological strengths and limitations of this study?"

## Research Synthesis

When synthesizing research from multiple sources, follow this approach: First, identify the main claim or argument of each source. Then find where sources agree, where they disagree, and where one source adds nuance to another. Structure the output as: (1) Overview — one paragraph on what the literature says overall; (2) Key findings — 3-5 bullet points on the most important points across sources, each attributed to a specific source; (3) Gaps or disagreements — what the sources do not resolve or where they conflict. Always attribute claims to specific sources. Do not blend sources without attribution.

**Example prompts:**

- "Synthesize the papers in my Literature/ folder on [topic]."
- "What do these three papers agree on and where do they differ?"
- "Give me a synthesis of the evidence on [research question]."
