## Ideation

When generating creative ideas, follow this approach: Do not filter ideas for practicality during generation — include unexpected directions alongside the practical ones. For each idea, provide a short, evocative name and a 2-3 sentence description of what it is and why it is interesting. Aim for genuine variety — if all ideas feel like variations on the same theme, generate more until you have options that are genuinely different from each other. Always include at least one idea that surprises you or challenges the obvious interpretation of the brief. After the ideas, ask: "Which direction interests you most?" — then develop from there.

**Example prompts:**

- "I'm working on [project]. Give me 5 different creative directions I could take it."
- "Generate 3 unexpected concepts for [brief or topic] — include one that surprises me."
- "What are some creative approaches to [problem] that haven't been done to death?"

## Creative Brief

When developing a creative brief, follow this approach: A brief exists to create aligned creative direction — not to constrain it. Ask for: (1) the problem being solved; (2) who the work is for (audience); (3) what success looks like; (4) any hard constraints (budget, format, platform, deadline). Then structure the brief with: Problem statement (1-2 sentences on what needs to be solved); Audience (who they are and what matters to them); Principles (2-4 creative principles that should guide the work — what it should feel like, not just what it should say); Constraints (non-negotiables: format, length, platform, tone); What success looks like (1-2 sentences on how to evaluate the work). Keep the brief tight — a brief that tries to say everything says nothing.

**Example prompts:**

- "Help me write a creative brief for [project or campaign]."
- "I have a vague brief from a client — help me sharpen it: [paste brief]."
- "What principles should guide this project? [describe context]."

## Feedback Synthesis

When synthesizing creative feedback from multiple sources, follow this approach: Read all feedback provided. Identify recurring themes — what do multiple reviewers mention? Also note outliers — strong reactions that only one person had but that seem worth addressing. Separate feedback into: (1) Clear signals — themes mentioned by 2 or more reviewers or rated highly; (2) Outliers — individual reactions that may or may not represent the broader audience; (3) Contradictions — where reviewers disagree and why the disagreement might matter. End with a recommended direction: given the feedback, what are the 1-2 most important things to address in the next iteration? Do not try to please everyone — identify the core signal and recommend action.

**Example prompts:**

- "Synthesize this feedback from 3 reviewers: [paste feedback]."
- "I got conflicting feedback on [work]. What's the signal and what's the noise?"
- "What should I prioritize in the next iteration based on this feedback? [paste]."
