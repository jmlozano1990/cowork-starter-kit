"Using this approach: [paste skill content] — now help me with [your task]."

## Voice Matching

When writing in the user's voice, follow this approach: First, read 2-3 samples from the user's Voice-and-Style/ folder or any previously shared writing. Identify: (1) sentence length patterns — short and punchy or long and flowing; (2) vocabulary level — casual or formal; (3) structural habits — does the writer use a lot of subheadings? numbered lists? long paragraphs? (4) tone — warm, authoritative, ironic, conversational. Then write the requested piece applying those patterns. Do not average the style out into generic "clear writing" — match the specific patterns you observed. After writing, note one sentence about the voice choices you made so the user can correct you if needed.

**Example prompts:**

- "Read my Voice-and-Style/ folder and write a 200-word intro for an article on [topic]."
- "Write in my voice: I need a LinkedIn post about [topic]."
- "Here are 3 examples of my writing: [paste]. Now write a 150-word section on [topic] in the same style."

## Editing Pass

When editing a draft, follow this approach: First, ask the user whether they want light edits (fix errors only), medium edits (improve clarity and flow), or heavy edits (restructure and rewrite). Do not assume the level. For light edits: flag only grammar, punctuation, and factual errors. For medium edits: also flag unclear sentences, awkward transitions, and weak word choices — suggest specific replacements. For heavy edits: restructure as needed, explain the structural choices, and flag anything that seems inconsistent with the user's stated intent. Always output: (1) a numbered list of specific suggestions, (2) the revised passage if you made changes, (3) a brief note on what you did and did not change. Do not rewrite the user's voice out of their work.

**Example prompts:**

- "Give this draft a medium edit pass: [paste or filename]."
- "What are the 3 weakest sentences in this piece and how would you fix them?"
- "Edit for clarity only — don't change the voice or structure."

## Outline Generator

When generating an outline, follow this approach: Ask the user for: (1) the type of content (article, essay, report, newsletter, etc.); (2) the target length; (3) the audience; (4) the main argument or takeaway. If any of these are missing, ask before proceeding. Then produce a structured outline with: a headline, a lead (1-2 sentences on the hook), section headers with 1-sentence descriptions of each section, and a closing direction. For essays: include the thesis statement. For listicles: include the list items. For newsletters: include the subject line and CTA. The outline should be specific enough that the user could write the piece from it without needing further clarification.

**Example prompts:**

- "Outline an 800-word article on [topic] for [audience]."
- "Help me outline my essay argument: I want to argue that [claim]."
- "Give me 3 different structural approaches for a piece on [topic]."
